Stop 4 (360° Panorama)
Old Pueblo Mine No. 5 Shaft Ruins

At Stop 4 we find ourselves once more standing atop the ruins of a mine shaft. You should be able to see a lot more of the light blue chrysocolla here. You should be able to see more, thicker, mineral veins.

Here we can see the answer for why a mine was built here near Starr Pass.

If we look around the rocks scattered on the ground here, we may be able to find small pieces of the rocks the miners really wanted. They were looking for pieces of milky white quartz. You can still see small quartz veins in the rocks here if you look carefully. The miners were hoping the quartz contained gold along with the copper.

From the various shafts and drifts here at the Old Pueblo Mine, miners removed 67 tons of ore. They ignored the white-speckled andesite porphyry and instead focused on the mineral veins containing quartz and chrysocolla. The mine produced copper, silver, and a small amount of gold. But 67 tons is not a lot of ore for a mine. A Tucson city garbage truck can hold around 12 tons. So in 6 trips the city could remove all the ore ever mined here. Compare that to the SIlverbell mine north of here, where one haul truck can hold 240 tons. So while there was ore here, there was not enough to sustain the mine for more than a few years.

For the final stop, let's leave the mine road and head up to the summit of Mine Mountain to see other ways Tucsonans interact with the Tucson Mountains.
